Introduction

I was recommended to Dan the CTO of Medopad by a developer I had worked with on a previous project.

Medopad are doing some really interesting things in healthcare by connecting hospitals and doctors directly to patients through the use of mobile technology.

The company has always been quick to adopt new technology and my brief was to create an Apple Watch app to coincide with the first release of the Apple Watch in June 2015.

The Problem

The ability to effectively manage cancer treatment relies heavily on strict drug regimens. These drugs can often cause adverse side-effects which result in the patient not adhering to their schedules and missing much needed medication.

For patients, communicating these side-effects to doctors is a pain and unless a patient has a direct line of communication to their physician, the lapses in drug adherence can go unnoticed until the next hospital visit.

Research

I use both iOS and Android devices in the my day to day so I rely on that familarity to make sure I am up to date with current design patterns, navigation structures and interactions on each of the patforms.

Apple Watch was a completely new device, introducing new ways to interact along with new opportunites to capture information from the user. Because it was so new and because the public did not have access to the device yet there was no easy way to get familiar with the technology.

A quick visit to the Apple website to read through the Apple Watch documentation allowed me to learn about the new gestures and the digital crown.

It was also gave me the opportunity to learn about the different ways to access app content through notifications, glances and the watch apps themselves.

Having becoming familar with the documentation I worked with Medopad to define the scope of the first version of the app. It would allow patients to do four things:

  • Receive real-time alerts to remind patients which medication to take at what time and at what dosage.
  • Mark a medication as taken so their adherence can be measured and reported back to physicians.
  • Report any symptoms they experience after taking their medication.
  • Track their temperature throughout the day and report it back to their physician.

Notifications

Notifications are a powerful feature on the Apple Watch. They allow information to be presented to the user right where they need it.

The user initially receives a vibration to alert them they have received a notification. The user can then decide if they want to act on it by raising their wrist to view the Short Look which tells the user what the notification is about and which app it is from.

If they decide they want to know more, they keep their wrist raised and the notification turns into a Long Look which presents further information and allows the user to interact with the notification for example by marking a medication as taken.

Reporting symptoms

The ability to report any symptoms experienced after taking medication is an important feature of the app. The design allowed for consumers to select from a list of potential symptoms and submit them to their doctor.

I conceived a design that allowed for three different states:

  • Initial state which allows the user to select the symptom.
  • Selected state which asks the user to confirm and submit to their doctor.
  • Confirmed state which shows the user that they have already selected that symptom.

The results

The app launch received some very positive feedback from both patients and doctors. The product continues to be developed and now has an accompanying iPhone app that mirrors a lot of the Watch apps' functionality.

This initial engagement led to an offer to go full-time with Medopad in a Product Management role which I respectfully declined.
